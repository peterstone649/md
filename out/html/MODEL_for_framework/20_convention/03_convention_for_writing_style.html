Core Writing Principles

Principle 1: Clarity Through Action

Source: "Style: Toward Clarity and Grace" (Williams) Framework Application: Transform abstract philosophical concepts into active, demonstrable actions

Character-Action-Subject Strategy

  1. Identify the Action - Determine what is actually happening in the concept
  2. Name the Actor - Specify who or what performs the action
  3. Position Actor First - Place the subject at the beginning of sentences

Framework Example: - Unclear: "The validation of the theorem was performed by the framework" - Clear: "The framework validates theorems through systematic verification"

Active Voice Preference

  • Preferred: Use active voice to show clear agency
  • Framework Rule: Use active voice in 80% of sentences unless passive is required for emphasis
  • Exception: Passive voice acceptable when actor is unknown or irrelevant

Principle 2: Concision and Economy

Source: "The Elements of Style" (Strunk & White), Rule 17 Framework Application: Eliminate unnecessary words while preserving philosophical precision

Omit Needless Words

  • Target: Remove redundant phrases and unnecessary modifiers
  • Framework Rule: Each word must contribute unique meaning or clarity
  • Validation: Can any word be removed without losing essential meaning?

Examples: - Wordy: "The framework provides a systematic approach to understanding complex philosophical concepts that have been developed over many years" - Concise: "Over many years, philosophers have developed complex concepts. Our framework provides a systematic approach to understanding them"

Nominalization Elimination

  • Issue: Converting verbs to nouns reduces clarity and action
  • Solution: Convert nouns back to verbs when possible
  • Framework Rule: Avoid unnecessary nominalizations in technical explanations

Principle 3: Coherence and Flow

Source: "Style: Toward Clarity and Grace" (Williams) Framework Application: Ensure logical progression in complex philosophical arguments

Old-to-New Information Flow

  • Old Information: What readers already know
  • New Information: What readers are learning
  • Framework Rule: Connect old information to new information smoothly

Transitional Elements

  • Required: Use explicit connectors between related ideas
  • Types: Logical connectors (therefore, however, consequently)
  • Framework Rule: Every paragraph transition must be explicit

Principle 4: Precision and Propriety

Source: "The Elements of Style" (Strunk & White) Framework Application: Use precise language appropriate for philosophical and technical content

Definite, Specific, Concrete Language

  • Preferred: Concrete terms over abstract generalizations
  • Framework Rule: Define abstract terms with concrete examples
  • Validation: Can the concept be illustrated with a specific example?

Proper Case and Agreement

  • Subject-Verb Agreement: Number agreement between subject and verb
  • Pronoun Case: Correct use of nominative and objective cases
  • Framework Rule: All grammatical rules apply unless clarity demands exception

Framework-Specific Writing Standards

Philosophical Writing Conventions

Theorem Presentations

  • Structure: State theorem, provide proof sketch, give concrete example
  • Clarity: Use active voice to show logical operations
  • Precision: Define all technical terms on first use

Axiom Descriptions

  • Format: State axiom, explain necessity, provide justification
  • Action: Show axioms "establishing" relationships rather than being "established"
  • Flow: Connect axioms to their consequences immediately

Conceptual Definitions

  • Method: Define by function before form
  • Examples: Provide concrete illustrations of abstract concepts
  • Relationships: Explicitly state how concepts interrelate

Technical Documentation Standards

Process Descriptions

  • Active Agents: Name who performs each step
  • Sequential Logic: Use clear temporal connectors (first, then, finally)
  • Validation Points: Include verification steps in process descriptions

System Architecture

  • Component Actions: Describe what components do, not what they are
  • Data Flow: Show how information moves through active processes
  • Integration Points: Clearly identify how components interact
