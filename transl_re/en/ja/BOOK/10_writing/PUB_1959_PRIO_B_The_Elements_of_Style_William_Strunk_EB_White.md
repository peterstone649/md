# The Elements of Style - William Strunk Jr. & E.B. White (1959)

## Book Overview

- **Publication**: 1959 (4th Edition including updates by E.B. White)
- **Original Author**: William Strunk Jr. (1918)
- **Reviser**: E.B. White (1959)
- **Pages**: 105
- **Genre**: Writing Guide, Style Manual
- **Impact**: One of the most influential writing guides in English, millions of copies sold
- **Kindle URL**: https://www.amazon.com/Elements-Style-Fourth-William-Strunk/dp/020530902X

## Historical Background

### **Origins**
- **William Strunk Jr.**: Professor at Cornell University, wrote the original guide for students in 1918.
- **E.B. White**: Famous author (*Charlotte's Web*, *Stuart Little*), revised and expanded Strunk's work.
- **Evolution**: From classroom material to global bestseller.

### **Cultural Impact**
- Required reading in many schools and universities.
- Influenced generations of writers, journalists, and scholars.
- Often called the "Little Book" due to its concise format.
- Translated into multiple languages.

## Core Philosophy

### **Basic Principles**
1. **Clarity First**: Clear writing is the ultimate goal.
2. **Simplicity**: Use simple words and structures.
3. **Directness**: Say what you want to say directly.
4. **Economy**: Omit needless words.

- Good writing requires both talent and learned skill.

## Key Rules and Principles

### **Elementary Rules of Usage**

#### **1. Form the possessive singular of nouns by adding 's**
- Correct: "the student's book"
- Incorrect: "the student book"
- Exception: Classical names (Jesus', Moses')

#### **2. In a series of three or more terms with a single conjunction, use a comma after each term except the last**
- Correct: "red, white, and blue"
- Incorrect: "red, white and blue"

#### **3. Enclose parenthetic expressions between commas**
- Correct: "The best way, of course, is to practice."
- Incorrect: "The best way of course is to practice."

#### **4. Place a comma before a conjunction introducing an independent clause**
- Correct: "He got up, and he left."
- Incorrect: "He got up and he left."

#### **5. Do not join independent clauses with a comma**
- Correct: "He got up and left." (single subject)
- Incorrect: "It was cold, he went inside." (comma splice)

#### **6. Do not break sentences in two**
- Correct: "I believe we can do it."
- Incorrect: "I believe. We can do it."

#### **7. Use the active voice**
- Recommended: "The committee approved the plan."
- Avoid: "The plan was approved by the committee."

#### **8. Use a colon after an independent clause to introduce a list of particulars**
- Correct: "We need: flour, sugar, and eggs."
- Incorrect: "We need, flour, sugar, and eggs."

#### **9. Use a dash to set off an abrupt break or interruption**
- Correct: "We were—surprise!—the winners."
- Incorrect: "We were (surprise!) the winners."

#### **10. The number of the subject determines the number of the verb**
- Correct: "The committee approves the plan."
- Incorrect: "The committee approve the plan."

#### **11. Use the proper case of pronouns**
- Correct: "The plan is for you and me."
- Incorrect: "The plan is for you and I."

### **Elementary Principles of Composition**

#### **12. Choose a suitable design and stick to it**
- Plan the structure before writing.
- Maintain consistent organization.
- Choose a form appropriate to the content.

#### **13. Make the paragraph the unit of composition**
- Each paragraph should have unity and coherence.
- Start with a topic sentence or appropriate transition.
- Develop one main idea per paragraph.

#### **14. Use the active voice**
- Emphasize the doer of the action.
- Makes writing more direct and forceful.
- Avoid passive structures when possible.

#### **15. Put statements in positive form**
- Recommended: "He was frugal."
- Avoid: "He was not extravagant."

#### **16. Use definite, specific, concrete language**
- Recommended: "The house had twelve rooms."
- Avoid: "The house was large."

#### **17. Omit needless words**
- Recommended: "He arrived on time."
- Avoid: "He arrived in a timely manner."

#### **18. Avoid a succession of loose sentences**
- Mix sentence structures for variety.
- Combine short sentences where appropriate.
- Use transitions between ideas.

#### **19. Express coordinate ideas in similar form**
- Parallel structure: "He came, he saw, he conquered."
- Do not mix forms inappropriately.

#### **20. Keep related words together**
- Place modifiers close to what they modify.
- Do not separate subject and verb.
- Maintain logical word order.

#### **21. In summaries, keep to one tense**
- Choose an appropriate tense and maintain consistency.
- Do not change tenses without reason.

#### **22. Place the emphatic words of a sentence at the end**
- English sentence structure builds to a climax.
- Save important words for the end of the sentence.

## Attitudes Toward Language

### **Attitude Toward Rules**
- Rules are servants, not masters.
- Break rules when clarity demands it.
- Rules exist to prevent common errors.
- Good usage changes over time.

### **Style Principles**
- **Conciseness**: Say more with fewer words.
- **Clarity**: Make meaning immediately obvious.
- **Directness**: Avoid circumlocution.
- **Sincerity**: Write what you mean.
- **Propriety**: Follow established conventions of usage.

### **Word Choice**
- Favor simple words over sophisticated ones.
- Use concrete terms rather than abstract ones.
- Choose Anglo-Saxon words over Latinate ones when possible.
- Avoid jargon unless necessary.

## Critical Reception

### **Strengths**
- **Conciseness**: Maximum impact in minimum space.
- **Practicality**: Rules based on actual writing problems.
- **Authority**: Backed by Strunk's teaching experience.
- **Permanence**: Principles remain relevant today.

### **Criticisms**
- **Prescriptivism**: Some rules seen as too rigid.
- **Limited Scope**: Focused on mechanics, less on creative aspects.
- **Cultural Bias**: Reflects mid-20th century American norms.
- **Oversimplification**: Reduces complex issues to rules.

### **Enduring Influence**
- Required text in many writing courses.
- Influenced global style guides.
- Sells well despite its age.
- Foundation of modern writing instruction.

## Conclusion

"The Elements of Style" remains one of the most influential writing guides ever published. Its concise and practical approach to clear writing continues to shape how English is written and taught worldwide. While some rules may seem dated, its core philosophy of clarity, simplicity, and directness is eternal.

The success of the book lies in its focus on the fundamentals: good writing is clear, concise, and direct. These principles transcend time, technology, and culture. Strunk and White's little book continues to teach generations of writers that clarity is not just a virtue but the foundation of effective communication.
